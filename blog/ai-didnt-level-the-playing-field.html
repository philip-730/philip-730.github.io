AI Didn't Level the Playing Field. It Steepened It.

On force multipliers, game theory, and the real cost of replacing expertise with automation.

Philip Amendolia April 2026

The Argument

Everyone has AI. You are not special for having AI. Your competitor has it. Their intern has it. Tons of people smarter than you and better businesses than yours have it. It's not going to be what makes you competitive.

When a capability becomes universally available, it stops being a differentiator and becomes a baseline. The internet didn't give every business equal footing — it gave every business the same ground floor, and then the race resumed. AI is doing the same thing, faster, and the people who don't understand this are the same people who thought buying enterprise software was a strategy.

Here's the thing nobody is saying out loud: AI raises the floor a little and the ceiling a lot. Yes, a junior hire with AI can do things they couldn't do before. The floor came up. But the improvement is not linear — it's multiplicative. Output is a function of skill, scaled by AI leverage. When you raise the leverage, you raise everyone's output, but you raise it proportionally to the skill they started with. The floor rose an inch. The ceiling rose a mile.

AI didn't remove the advantage of being good. It made the advantage of being good unreasonably large.

The person "vibe coding their startup killer" on LinkedIn didn't succeed because AI removed the need for expertise. They succeeded because they had ten years of knowing what to build, who needs it, and what good looks like — and AI let them execute without a team. The expertise wasn't "how to code." It was judgment: what to build, what to skip, when the output was wrong. AI handled the execution. The human handled every decision that made the execution worth anything. In no version of that story does the order reverse.

A senior engineer using AI is not the same as a junior engineer using AI. A strategist with fifteen years of client work using AI is not the same as a coordinator who took a prompt engineering course. A lead designer using AI is not the same as someone who learned Figma last month. You are not comparing equals. You are comparing a scalpel with a handle to a scalpel duct-taped to a stick.

At low skill levels, AI helps — that's the floor rising. A mediocre hire with AI produces slightly better output than they would have alone. But there's not much to multiply. Congratulations, you've achieved mediocrity at scale.

Watch what happens at the right side of that chart. The gap doesn't converge — it accelerates. High-skill workers in the AI era don't just outperform their pre-AI selves. They outperform their low-skill AI-equipped competitors by a margin that grows with every improvement to the underlying model.

Every time the model gets better, the gap gets wider. This is not a bug. It's the math.

The Real Threat Nobody Talks About

You didn't lower the barrier to compete. You lowered it for everyone — including your ex-employees.

Think about what you actually did when you replaced your experienced team with cheaper people and an AI subscription. You took people who understood your domain, your clients, your processes, and your failure modes, and you gave them a severance package and six months of runway. You handed them domain expertise, freed them from your org chart, and left them with full access to every tool you're using.

They have no overhead. No office. No executive team approving roadmaps. No re-org every eighteen months. No all-hands about culture. They have a laptop and ten years of knowing exactly where the bodies are buried in your industry.

You didn't disrupt your industry. You trained your future competitors and paid for their launch.

The calculus has always been this: the cost of a high-skill hire is high, but the cost of a high-skill hire who goes and competes against you is higher. The difference is that before AI, it was hard to go from "laid-off expert" to "running shop" fast enough to matter. Now the friction is gone. The tools are free. The cloud is pay-as-you-go. The time from "I got the call" to "I have a customer" is a weekend.

You optimized your headcount and created a competitor who understands your business better than your new hires ever will.

The scores are directional, not measured. The relative payoffs are the argument, not the specific values.

"Keep skilled people" is the dominant strategy in any market where quality exists on a spectrum — which is most markets. If your competitor keeps talent, you're competitive. If they cut, you win outright. Cutting is only rational if you have already decided to be mediocre.

The only honest counterargument: some markets have a quality ceiling, where output beyond a certain point doesn't convert to more revenue. Competent marketing execution for a mid-market company. Routine legal document review. Standard financial reporting. Work where "good enough" is genuinely good enough and the client cannot tell the difference between competent and exceptional. If AI can meet that ceiling without skilled people, the math appears to change.

It doesn't — and that +/+ in the bottom-right corner of the matrix is a mirage. It looks survivable in the short term: both sides cut costs, margins compress, everyone stays in business. But you are not competing with the other company that also cut. You are competing with the software itself — a product that scales infinitely, doesn't take vacation, and will undercut both of you by an order of magnitude. That +/+ is not an equilibrium. It's a countdown. The businesses trying to win a ceiling-bound market by hiring cheaper humans are just a slower, more expensive version of the software — and the software always wins on price.

The right response to a ceiling-bound market is not to deskill. It's to recognize that the floor of your market just became the ceiling, and either climb the value stack or get out. The companies that cut skilled people to meet the threshold aren't being strategic — they're buying themselves a few quarters before the software replaces them entirely.

What the Incentives Actually Say

The incentive to hire smart people didn't go away. It accelerated.

Your best person with AI doesn't just get faster — their leverage compounds. Every decision they make — what to prioritize, what to cut, how to position, when to push back on a client — is now backed by a tool that can hold the entire context of a problem in working memory, generate alternatives in seconds, and surface edge cases they might have taken a day to find on their own. The improvement is not additive. It is multiplicative, and it compounds on the skill that was already there.

A mediocre hire with AI is a mediocre hire who moves faster. The AI cannot compensate for not knowing what to ask, not recognizing when an answer is wrong, not understanding the second-order effects of a decision, and not having the judgment to know when to stop. These are skill problems. AI doesn't fix skill problems. It amplifies whatever is already there.

Every capability improvement in the underlying models — better reasoning, longer context, broader domain knowledge — increases the return on hiring someone who actually understands what they're doing. The smarter the AI gets, the more the expert can leverage it, and the more the non-expert is exposed by it.

Cutting to cheap plus AI is like removing the RAM from your computer because you bought better software. The software requires the RAM. That's the whole point.

The people selling you the "AI replaces experts" story are either selling you AI tools or they don't understand what their experts were actually doing. Possibly both. If you believe it — congratulations, your mind has been colonized. The work of a senior person is not "produce deliverables faster." It's judgment, pattern recognition, taste, and the ability to see three problems ahead. AI can help with all of those — but only if someone is holding the other end.

The bar hasn't been lowered. The ceiling has been raised.

If you fired your smart people because you thought AI made them replaceable, you didn't cut costs. You handed your competitors a head start, trained your future rivals on your own dime, and downgraded your team's output at exactly the moment when the gap between good and great is wider than it has ever been.

The models will keep improving. The multiplier will keep climbing. And every improvement will widen the gap between the teams that have someone who knows what to point the AI at and the teams that don't. This is not a correction that reverses. It is a divergence that accelerates.

You are on the wrong side of it.
